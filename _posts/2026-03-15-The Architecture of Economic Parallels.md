"History repeats itself" but how do we know when two historical periods are truly comparable? [This paper](https://www.adb.org/publications/renminbi-revaluation-lessons-and-experiences?__cf_chl_tk=GVEe7V2V2vn.fiQalAmT8Me5Vd1Q5oDW.H77ccyi0BE-1773828208-1.0.1.1-LU8r81xvyXSx3WXgWcj9V9tTGlnSumuNqs3fhJaVLCs) examines the Asian Development Bank's framework for comparing Japan's sharp yen appreciation in the 1970s through the mid-to-late 1980s with China's renminbi situation in 2005. By reconstructing the logic of that comparison, we propose a generalizable regime model for evaluating cross-country, cross-period economic parallels.

## I. The Central Problem: Similarity Across Time
Comparative economic history rests on an uncomfortable assumption: two different countries, in two different eras, can be "similar enough" that one's experience generates actionable lessons for the other.

But it is often not very evident to recognize the similarity. It must be constructed through a deliberate analytical framework. The ADB's comparison of Japan and China gives a great groundwork to build the analytical tool. The "theme variable" is sharp currency appreciation under a pegged exchange rate amid export-driven growth. Everything else follows from that anchor.

## II. Why the ADB Chose Japan
Before asking what China can learn from Japan, we must ask why Japan was chosen as the reference case at all. The answer lies in a specific chain of structural logic.

### The Impossible Trinity as the Starting Point
Both Japan in the early 1970s and China in 2005 were caught in the same fundamental macroeconomic trap: the **Mundell-Fleming Impossible Trinity**. A country cannot simultaneously maintain a fixed exchange rate, an independent monetary policy, and free capital flows. Both economies had built their growth models on export dependency and a pegged currency, which meant that as trade surpluses accumulated and foreign capital flowed in, the pressure on the peg became unsustainable.

Japan's response was to keep buying dollars to defend the peg. China in 2005 was doing precisely the same thing: absorbing U.S. dollars to stabilize the renminbi, sacrificing domestic monetary equilibrium in service of external stability.

### The Structural Preconditions That Match
The ADB identified four core structural parallels that justified the comparison:

1.  **Pegged currency under downward dollar pressure.** Japan maintained a fixed rate of ¥360/USD under Bretton Woods until 1971. China maintained an 8.28 peg from 1994 until 2005. In both cases, the U.S. dollar's declining real value made the peg increasingly artificial and politically contentious.
2.  **Export dependency and trade surplus accumulation.** Both economies functioned as the world's primary low-cost manufacturing base at the time of comparison.
3.  **Foreign reserve accumulation.** Defending the peg required both central banks to absorb massive dollar inflows, building up foreign reserves at a scale that itself became a source of macroeconomic distortion.
4.  **U.S. external pressure to revalue.** Partially politically demanded by the U.S.

## III. The Surrounding Variables
The surrounding variables are the factors that differ between cases.

### China's Structural Differences from Japan
* **Capital account and FDI.** By the time Japan faced its most severe appreciation pressure in the mid-1980s, it had substantially liberalized its capital account. China in 2005 retained heavy capital controls. Moreover, China's integration into the global economy was far more FDI-driven than Japan's had been, meaning the capital account surplus was structurally larger and more entangled with foreign production networks. This gave China both greater vulnerability (larger inflows) and greater policy tools (controls that Japan had already relinquished).
* **The unemployment constraint.** Japan did not face a structural rural unemployment crisis during its appreciation period. China in 2005 had hundreds of millions of rural workers whose livelihoods depended on labor-intensive export industries. A sharp, rapid appreciation would have devastated these sectors.
* **Non-performing loans in state-owned banks.** Japan's banking crisis emerged after the bubble burst. China in 2005 was carrying it before any bubble had fully formed.
* **Sectoral exposure.** Specific industries like agriculture and automobiles faced asymmetric exposure to renminbi appreciation

These were the pressure points of China compared to Japan.

## IV. What PRC Can Learn
The paper emphasized the lessons on the structural parallels and where the surrounding variables diverge:

### On exchange rate adjustment:
Japan's experience confirms that external imbalance cannot be resolved through exchange rate adjustment alone. Currency appreciation raises export prices in USD, compressing competitiveness, but does not automatically rebalance the current account if domestic demand remains suppressed and industrial structure remains unchanged. China needed to pair any appreciation with genuine expansion of domestic consumption.

### On monetary policy independence:
The Impossible Trinity demands a choice. Japan's attempt to have all three of them (fixed rate, open capital account, independent monetary policy) ultimately collapsed. China's capital controls gave it a temporary escape from this trilemma, but the ADB framework suggests this was a window of opportunity, not a permanent solution. China should use the window of capital controls to build the institutional infrastructure for a market-determined exchange rate before being forced into one.

Ultimately, China should build a flexible exchange rate regime with a gradualist principle and let the FX market explore its equilibrium level.

## V. Can We Generalize at All?
This brings us to the deepest question the framework raises. Are we entitled to generalize a process of currency appreciation and its macroeconomic consequences across countries and time periods?

### The Middle Position
* **Pure historicism:** “Every case is unique, nothing generalizes.”
* **Naive cyclicalism:** “History always repeats.”

What can be reliably generalized is the set of structural preconditions that elevate the probability of a crisis: currency misalignment, capital flow surges into asset markets, and monetary policy that prioritizes external over internal balance. What cannot be generalized is the trigger, timing, or severity of any particular outcome that are tied to institutions, politics, and accidents.

The ADB framework implicitly operates at this level. It does not predict that China will repeat Japan's lost decade. It identifies the structural pressure points where China is vulnerable, maps the surrounding variables that give China policy room Japan lacked, and derives conditional lessons.

## VI. Conclusion
What the Japan-China comparison ultimately offers is not a prediction but a framework:
1.  **Fix a theme variable**
2.  **Match structural preconditions:** Start from Mundell-Fleming Impossible Trinity
3.  **Map surrounding variables:** Identify where cases differ, and in which policy-relevant direction
4.  **Assess institutional capacity:** Determine policy tools and each country’s pressure points
5.  **Derive conditional lessons:** Not "do what they did" but "given that Z differs, instrument W is available that the reference case lacked"

This framework does not resolve the tension between historicism and cyclicalism. History does not repeat itself exactly. But the structural conditions that make certain outcomes probable recur with enough regularity that careful comparative analysis remains one of the most valuable tools available to economists and policymakers alike.

The question is never whether two historical moments are identical. It is whether they are similar enough, in the right ways, to make the comparison illuminating rather than misleading.
