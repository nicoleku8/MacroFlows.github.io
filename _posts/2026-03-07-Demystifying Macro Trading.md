Trading often feels remarkably vague. Despite the mountains of literature on investor psychology, asset pricing, and derivatives, there is a persistent gap between understanding the news and executing a strategy. You can read the *Wall Street Journal* and grasp the global narrative, yet still struggle to build a clear logical chain explaining a sudden asset plunge or predicting which specific companies will succumb to a sector-wide supply chain crisis.

Without a robust framework to synthesize information into a "bigger picture," it is nearly impossible to transform raw data into a viable strategy.

The hardest part for me has been gaining confidence in my research and determining how a strategy fits into the broader market flow. Even with a great analysis of a company or a financial product, I recognize that if I fail to account for current macro momentum, the strategy is at high risk of losing money. Books like *If It's Raining in Brazil, Buy Starbucks* provided me with a strong foundation for this **Top-Down** trading approach.

### Navigating Systematic Doubts
In building systematic strategies, several doubts often slow the pace of a project:

1. How much can we truly trust certain data trends?
2. If we apply neural networks or deep learning to find hidden patterns, can we even assume a consistent pattern exists in the market?
3. If not, how can we interpret this data statistically through a clear, prudent process?
4. With a profit hypothesis, how can I ensure I am not biased toward the "existence" of the strategy? How do we find truly statistically significant analysis?
5. If we find a signal through technical analysis, how does it differ from signals used by discretionary traders, and how can we be sure of its future validity?

### Core Principles
After reading numerous podcasts and books on trading (e.g. macro trading, options trading, ex-hedge fund employees talking about how they found alpha/edge, derivatives sales trading), I found common factors that they say about how to survive in this seems to be a hyper-competitive industry. Regardless of who they are, I always found this advice from them. 

## 1. Read Books
Compound knowledge and establish your domain, find signals and remember them like you're storing them in a library of historical situations and analogies. This was repeated over and over with my supervisor from internship telling me that **past regimes repeat again and again**. This is where a trader’s intuition and market understanding are born.

## 2. Be able to bet on incomplete information
I once hesitated to trade real money, feeling it was akin to gambling. However, the most important part is building a stringent process to verify decisions. And quantitatively, it is equivalent to making your expected return to be positive. 


## 3. Understand every tick of the market
Consider the market as a living organism, constantly shifting but always having a fundamental nature. Understand them and their volatility, patterns, players in the market, and know what you know and don’t know.

### Strategic Framework
Now, next doubts would be following: 

**When we build a project:** 

1. How can I create structured thinking and a discipline to translate information to a strategy? 
2. How can I build a discipline to be rigidly sophisticated in choosing parameters?
3. How can I dissect every element until I see a structure behind chaos? 
4. After I build a strategy, if someone asks me my design choices with 5 consecutive whys, am I confident enough to answer with a deeper level of logic? 

I think the first step is to understand the **BIG FLOW** of the market. John Tudor Jones famously said, *"The whole world is simply nothing more than a flow chart for capital."*

After clarifying my sources of doubts, I found a focus on what I need to do to think as a trader. It’s like matching puzzles with different information while recognizing the existence of all different puzzle pieces. 

Thus, the next step was identifying the big flow. In this environment, even a seemingly unrelated article in the *Financial Times* can serve as the final piece of a complex puzzle, fundamentally shifting the narrative of the market I am exploring. I’ve come to realize that thinking like a trader is a process of high-level pattern matching. It requires synthesizing disparate streams of information while recognizing that every individual piece exists within a larger, interconnected system.

### Conclusion: Chess and Poker
I long struggled to articulate exactly why I am drawn to the markets. I knew my interest stemmed from a desire to analyze the "Big Flow" but I worried that explanation wasn't "convincing" enough for others. Everything clicked when I encountered an [interview](https://cdo.mit.edu/blog/2024/12/19/career-stories-george-wu-mfin-25-part-1-breaking-into-quant-trading-insights-skills-and-interview-tips/) with a macro quant trader who compared the field to a mixture of **Chess and Poker**.

* **The Chess:** The deep, structural logic required to build a strategy and anticipate several moves ahead.
* **The Poker:** The probabilistic nature of the market, where you must make high-stakes decisions based on incomplete information and human psychology.

For me, the most compelling aspect of this field is the synthesis of these two worlds. Analyzing the "small components" like the motivations of Federal Reserve directors, the maneuvers of politicians, and the sentiment of retail investors and seeing those human relationships "scored" by PnL is an incredibly motivating challenge. 

Beyond the intellectual thrill and the competitive compensation, I have found traders to be exceptionally sharp, intellectually motivated, and possessed of a unique clarity of judgment. These are the people I want to be surrounded by.
